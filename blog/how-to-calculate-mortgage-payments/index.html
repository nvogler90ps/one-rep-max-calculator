How to Calculate Mortgage Payments: The Math Behind Your Monthly Bill

By Nicholas Vogler -- March 13, 2026 -- 5 min read

When you take out a mortgage, your lender uses a specific formula to determine your monthly payment. Understanding this formula helps you evaluate offers, plan your budget, and see exactly how interest rates and loan terms affect what you pay. This guide walks through the math step by step, with real examples you can follow along with.

The Mortgage Payment Formula

The standard formula for calculating a fixed-rate mortgage payment is:

M = P × [r(1 + r)n] / [(1 + r)n - 1]

Where:

  • M = monthly payment (principal and interest only)
  • P = loan principal (the amount you borrow)
  • r = monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12)
  • n = total number of payments (loan term in years times 12)

This formula produces an amortized payment -- the same fixed dollar amount every month for the life of the loan. Early in the loan, most of your payment goes toward interest. Over time, more goes toward principal. The formula ensures the loan is fully paid off by the end of the term.

A Worked Example: $300,000 Loan at 7%

Say you are borrowing $300,000 at a 7% annual interest rate on a 30-year fixed mortgage.

Step 1: Convert the rate

Monthly interest rate: 7% / 12 = 0.5833% = 0.005833

Step 2: Calculate total payments

30 years times 12 months = 360 payments

Step 3: Plug into the formula

M = 300,000 × [0.005833 × (1.005833)360] / [(1.005833)360 - 1]

(1.005833)360 = 8.1165 (approximately)

M = 300,000 × [0.005833 × 8.1165] / [8.1165 - 1]

M = 300,000 × 0.04735 / 7.1165

M = 300,000 × 0.006653

M = $1,996 per month (principal and interest)

Over 30 years, you will pay $1,996 × 360 = $718,527 total -- meaning $418,527 goes to interest alone. That is nearly 1.4 times the original loan amount in interest.

  2. A 15-year term costs more monthly but saves a massive amount on interest. At 7%, the 15-year loan saves over $233,000 in total interest compared to the 30-year option.

What the Formula Does Not Include

The standard mortgage formula only calculates principal and interest (P&I). Your actual monthly bill will also include:

  • Property taxes -- varies by location, typically 0.5-2.5% of home value per year
  • Homeowner's insurance -- usually $1,000-$3,000 per year
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI) -- required if your down payment is less than 20%, typically 0.5-1% of the loan per year
  • HOA fees -- if applicable, can range from $100 to $1,000+ per month

Strategies to Lower Your Monthly Payment

  • Make a larger down payment. Borrowing less means paying less. A 20% down payment also eliminates PMI.
  • Shop for lower rates. Even a 0.25% difference saves thousands over the life of a loan. Get quotes from at least 3 lenders.
  • Choose a longer term. A 30-year loan has lower monthly payments than a 15-year, though you pay more total interest.
  • Buy discount points. Paying 1% of the loan upfront (one "point") typically reduces your rate by 0.25%. This makes sense if you plan to keep the loan for many years.

When to Use a 15-Year vs. 30-Year Mortgage

A 15-year mortgage is better if you can comfortably afford the higher payment and want to build equity faster. A 30-year mortgage gives you more flexibility in your monthly budget. If you get a 30-year loan and make extra principal payments when you can, you get some of the interest savings with more flexibility.

Understanding Amortization

Amortization is how your payment splits between principal and interest over time. In the early years of a 30-year mortgage, roughly 70-80% of each payment goes to interest. By the final years, nearly all of it goes to principal.

This is why making extra payments early in the loan is so powerful -- each dollar of extra principal you pay now saves you multiple dollars of future interest. On our mortgage calculator, you can model extra payments and see exactly how much time and money they save.

The Bottom Line

The mortgage payment formula is straightforward once you break it down: your loan amount, interest rate, and loan term determine your payment. Small changes in rate or term have a big impact over 15 or 30 years. Before committing to a loan, run the numbers at different scenarios.
